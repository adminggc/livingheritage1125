Chinese medicine boosts regenerative medicine in stem cell-based therapy

Source: Stemcellres | 25 September 2025

Abstract

This review article explores the possible role of Chinese medicine (CM) in modulating stem cells for regenerative medicine, synthesizing evidence from animal experiments and human trials. The article focuses on how CM modulates the stem cell environment, specifically their roles in delaying cellular senescence and promoting stem cell survival, enhancing proliferation and differentiation, as well as stimulating exosome secretion.

It also conducts a critical analysis of methodological rigor and clinical transparency within the included studies to enable a more objective assessment of their reliability and reproducibility. To guarantee the responsible integration of CM and stem cells in future clinical application, it also discusses the safety, efficacy, and heterogeneity of stem cells, as well as delivery methods, alongside the dose–response relationship of CM.

The current evidence for CM in stem cell therapy remains constrained by the absence of standardized comparative baselines in animal studies and clinical outcome assessment. This methodological gap not only compromises the evidentiary weight of herbal effects but also introduces confounding variables in studies. The elucidation of CM’s mechanistic role in stem cell therapeutics necessitates robust interdisciplinary collaboration; this is an imperative and critically urgent need within a peer-reviewed research framework.

Introduction

Regenerative medicine is a biomedical discipline aimed at repair or replacement of damaged, diseased, or metabolically deficient organs, tissues, and cells via tissue engineering, cell transplantation, artificial organs or bioartificial organs and tissues.

Stem cell-based therapy is a branch of regenerative medicine, wherein stem cells are able to differentiate into various types of cells under specific conditions. Stem cells have shown broad prospects in the field of regenerative medicine. Through asymmetric division, they not only replenish the stem cell pool but also differentiate into tissue-specific cells to perform particular physiological functions in growth, development, repair, and regeneration. Their effectiveness in tissue repair and regeneration is remarkable, making them a hotspot in medical research.

Crucially, distinct stem cell lineages exhibit divergent biological properties that directly impact their clinical translation. Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) have long been controversial due to ethical concerns regarding their source, which limits their application in clinical research. Preclinical studies have found that ESCs lack immune privilege and are subject to immunological rejection. Additionally, the risk of malignancy further restricts ESC-based treatment.

Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) can be reprogrammed from adult somatic cells and differentiate into neural cells and cardiomyocytes, ushering in an era of personalized medicine. Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), characterized by abundant sources, low immunogenicity, and immunomodulatory properties, have become a vital component in regenerative medicine. Other adult stem cells such as intestinal stem cells (ISCs), hair follicle stem cells (HFSCs), and neural stem cells (NSCs) are responsible for growth and repair of specific tissues.

Given the obstacles in stem cell therapy, Chinese medicine (CM) could provide multi-target interventions to attenuate these effects. Existing studies have found that CM may support stem cell-based therapy by enhancing survival rate, promoting migration and retention, stimulating angiogenesis, reducing immune rejection, guiding directional differentiation, and improving proliferation and differentiation capabilities of stem cells.

Biological mechanisms and examples

CM is a complex system composed of multiple bioactive constituents. Modern research identifies active CM components including alkaloids (e.g., berberine, rhynchophylline, ephedrine), volatile oils (e.g., menthol, eugenol, camphor), and glycosides (e.g., ginsenosides, notoginsenosides, baicalin). These compounds can modulate stem cell behaviors such as proliferation, differentiation, anti-inflammation, and antioxidation.

Preclinical studies provide preliminary evidence for synergistic effects between CM and MSCs. For example, Ma et al. observed that combining BMSCs with the Bushen Huoxue Huazhuo formula in a liver fibrosis model enhanced BMSC colonization in liver tissue, reduced hepatocyte apoptosis, suppressed pathological injury, and decreased fibrosis markers compared with monotherapy.

A common limitation is the lack of mechanistic depth: many studies show associations between combined therapy and improved outcomes without defining molecular causation or specific interaction pathways between CM components and stem cells. Therefore, rigorously designed clinical trials are necessary to validate efficacy, safety, and mechanisms in humans.

CM modulates the stem cell environment

The stem cell environment includes the specific niche and systemic conditions that influence cell behavior. CM formulas show potential to modulate this environment by adjusting cytokines, growth factors, and immune status.

Clinical evidence suggests kidney-tonifying strategies may promote liver regeneration and repair in chronic hepatitis B-related liver failure, potentially via effects on stem cells and their microenvironment. The Diwu Yanggan compound has been shown to modulate IL-1, VEGF, and GRO/KC, thereby influencing the hepatic stem cell microenvironment in a rat injury model.

CM also exhibits systemic immunomodulatory properties that can enhance stem cell survival and improve the therapeutic milieu. In an allergic rhinitis model, MSCs treated with Qi-fang-Bi-min-tang reduced proinflammatory factors and increased splenic Treg proportions and plasma TGF-β1. In ischemia models, combined curcumin and hUC-MSC therapy suppressed proinflammatory cytokines and oxidative mediators while boosting antiinflammatory cytokines and antioxidant activity.

Growth factors

CM can upregulate key growth factors such as HGF, VEGF, FGF, IGF-1, PDGF, and BMP-2, which regulate proliferation, differentiation, and survival. For example, combining Lugua polypeptides with UC-MSCs improved rheumatoid arthritis outcomes by increasing HGF expression.

In myocardial infarction models, combining iPSC-derived therapies with a modified Taohong Siwu decoction improved cardiac function, reduced infarct size, and increased VEGF and FGF, potentially due to blood-activating and stasis-resolving effects that improve hemodynamics and cell retention.

CM delays stem cell senescence

Stem cell senescence involves epigenetic alterations, proteostasis dysfunction, ROS accumulation, and activation of p53/p21/p16 pathways. Growing evidence suggests CM can delay senescence by reducing ROS, activating Nrf2, regulating autophagy, and stabilizing organelle function such as mitochondria.

Examples include Guilu Erxian Jiao reducing p16, p53, and p21 expression in chemotherapy-induced senescent HSCs; Liuwei Dihuang Wan protecting BMSCs via the YAP–autophagy axis; Zuogui Wan activating Nrf2 in ovarian aging models; and Erxian decoction inducing plasma exosomes that rescued mitophagy in senescent BMSCs.

Proliferation & differentiation — key signaling pathways

CM promotes stem cell proliferation and differentiation through Wnt/β-catenin, PI3K/AKT, and MAPK/ERK pathways.

1. Wnt/β-catenin pathway

CM can activate Wnt signaling (e.g., increase Wnt3a) and stem cell markers (Lgr5, Sox2, Ki67, PCNA), supporting proliferation and differentiation across tissues. Berberine promoted stromal Wnt expression to aid mucosal repair; ginseng inhibited GSK-3β to stabilize β-catenin; oleanolic acid inhibited GSK-3β to drive NSC proliferation.

2. PI3K/AKT pathway

Many CM formulas and compounds (e.g., Astragalus, Gualou Xiebai Banxia, Ginsenoside Rg1) enhance PI3K/AKT phosphorylation, counteracting hypoxia-ischemia-induced apoptosis in BMSCs and promoting survival, proliferation, and beneficial autophagy.

3. MAPK/ERK pathway

MAPK/ERK signaling is a convergence point for CM compounds regulating proliferation, differentiation, and functional restoration in neurological, musculoskeletal, and gastrointestinal contexts (e.g., Astragaloside IV, berberine, naringin).

Homing, engraftment and survival of stem cells

CM supports homing and engraftment by modulating chemokines and receptors such as SCF and SDF-1/CXCR4. Preparations like Xuesaitong, Danhong injection, and Guanxin Danshen have been reported to increase SCF or SDF-1 and/or CXCR4 expression on MSCs, improving retention and repair capacity.

Compounds such as resveratrol improved survival and engraftment of hUC-MSCs in Alzheimer’s models with functional benefits. CM may also mitigate complications like GVHD via modulation of TGF-β1, BMPs, and NF-κB pathways to support cell survival.

CM and stem cell-derived exosomes (SC-Exos)

SC-Exos (30–150 nm) carry proteins, lipids, mRNAs, and miRNAs, mediating intercellular communication and playing key roles in tissue repair and immune regulation. CM can enhance SC-Exos secretion and alter cargo composition, enriching protective and antiinflammatory miRNAs (e.g., miR-146a-5p, miR-223-5p, miR-133a-3p, miR-138-5p).

Examples: Tongxinluo preconditioning enriched MSC exosomes with miR-146a-5p (targeting IRAK2 → reduced NF-κB); Tanshinone IIA increased miR-223-5p to inhibit CCR2 and reduce inflammation; Lycium barbarum polysaccharide enriched NSC-Exos with miR-133a-3p (activating AMPK/mTOR); catalpol promoted NSC exosomes with miR-138-5p to support neural survival.

Additional regulatory effects

CM also enhances tissue perfusion, modulates gene expression, influences cellular metabolism, and regulates intercellular interactions, thereby optimizing the stem cell niche. For instance, CM can increase microvessel density (CD31) around grafts, modulate Notch in cancer stem cells, or elevate spermidine to support eIF5A hypusination and activate dormant stem cells.

Challenges & future prospects

Although combining CM and stem cell therapy holds promise, three major challenges must be addressed:

  1. Theoretical and methodological differences: CM emphasizes individualized syndrome differentiation while stem cell therapy requires standardized protocols. New integrative frameworks are needed for clinical evaluation.
  2. Interdisciplinary collaboration gaps: A shortage of researchers proficient in both CM and stem cell biology hinders effective translational research; multidisciplinary teams are essential.
  3. Translational discrepancies: Much mechanistic research is in vitro or in animal models, whereas clinical delivery often uses systemic infusion. This mismatch may affect outcomes and complicate translation to human trials.

Future research should focus on pathologies where both CM and stem cells have mechanistic relevance, standardize protocols, optimize dosing, delivery routes and timing, and perform rigorous clinical trials to establish efficacy and safety.

Conclusion

Stem cell therapy holds significant promise for treating a wide spectrum of diseases and injuries. CM offers substantial potential to optimize this approach by modulating the stem cell environment, delaying senescence, promoting proliferation and differentiation, facilitating homing and engraftment, enhancing survival, stimulating exosome secretion, and exerting other beneficial effects. The strategic integration of CM with stem cell therapy therefore represents a compelling approach to improve treatment efficacy for various refractory conditions. While significant challenges remain, ongoing research provides encouraging progress. Future efforts must prioritize optimizing treatment protocols and conducting rigorous clinical validation to firmly establish the therapeutic advantages of this synergistic combination.
