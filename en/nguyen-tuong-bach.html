Heritage Characters

AUTHOR

NGUYEN TƯONG BACH

WISDOM

The first question for the author Nguyen Tuong Bach is clear: How can he observe with such profound subtlety, distill his findings into the very essence of living, and express it all with such effortless nature?

A man who emerged from Western science, yet possessed of an Eastern philosophical mind, who in turn became an author steeped in Buddhist insight—in him, intellect and lived experience merge to form a vast, fertile confluence.

To sit and listen to him speak is to take an odyssey straight into the spirit of one who has personally traversed continents and penetrated worlds, all to pen such luminous lines on human existence, the world, and the cosmos.

In Nguyen Tuong Bach, Wisdom (Hiền) is not merely a virtue; it is profound comprehension. It is insight that has fully blossomed into altruism.

Nguyen Tuong Bach (born 1948) is an erudite author, translator, and speaker, known for his rare ability to harmonize the wisdom of Eastern philosophy, the rigor of Western scientific thought, and a profound depth of inner reflection. Today, he stands as one of the most influential voices in contemporary Vietnamese spiritual life, admired for his philosophically rich writing and deeply humanitarian vision.

Education & Background:

  • Born in 1948 and raised in Hue.
  • Moved to Germany in 1967 and earned his PhD from the University of Stuttgart.
  • Nearly six decades of living and working in Europe have given him deep exposure to both Eastern and Western cultural traditions, shaping a uniquely international perspective.

Career & Contributions:

  • A respected translator of influential works such as The Tao of Physics, The Way of the White Clouds, Facing Life, and The Snow Lion’s Turquoise Mane.
  • He is also the author of numerous books beloved by Vietnamese intellectual readers, including Last Night, a Plum Blossom in the Front Courtyard, Who Wove the Sky’s Net?, The Scent of Agarwood, Endless Dream of Life, New Sunlight on a Far Road, The Vast Open Path, and others — works that inspire equanimity, mindfulness, and a deep appreciation for the contemplative spirit of Buddhism.

QUOTES

If one had to choose what makes the name Nguyễn Tường Bách most memorable, it would not be what he wrote, but how he lived: an unhurried adventure through life, free of judgment and dogma. And he leaves us with an invitation to experience it for ourselves: "The Mind (Tâm) of a human is miraculous. Begin to understand the Mind."

Having studied both Buddhism and science at such a profound level, where do you find the "essence" of life: in knowledge, in spiritual practice, in people, or in life and reality "as-it-is"?

There are many things one could call the “essence” of life: thought, art, science, philosophy, faith, love, material pleasure, family happiness. But to me, nothing is more precious in a human life than the “Knowing” of oneself — the knowing that comes from looking inward, from turning perception back upon itself; not looking outward, but directly into one’s own Tâm (Heart–Mind).

Human beings tend to look outward, taking the reactions of the external world as the measure of themselves. When something aligns with us, we feel joy; when it differs from us, we feel suffering. This is the source of both happiness and sorrow — though sorrow, for most people, is the greater part.

To look inward is the very heart of Zen Buddhism. In the West, too, they clearly understand that knowing one’s own Mind is the key that unlocks all wisdom. They say: “Know Yourself.”
